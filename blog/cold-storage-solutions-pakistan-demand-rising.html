Cold Storage Demand in Pakistan: 2026 Market Outlook

Published 2026-01-28 · Updated 2026-04-30 · By Izhar Foster Engineering · 8 min read

Pakistan's cold chain is at an inflection point. The market is projected to surpass USD 730 million by 2030, growing at 9–11% CAGR — yet the country still has roughly 0.07 m³ of cold storage per capita, less than one-fifth of India's level and a fraction of OECD norms. The gap between what Pakistan grows, manufactures, and imports — and what it can keep cold — has become the single largest constraint on agriculture, pharma, and food-export growth. Investors who build now will be filling demand that already exists; those who wait will be entering a crowded market in three years.

The numbers behind the demand

Three statistics tell the story. First, Pakistan loses 30–40% of its fruit and vegetable harvest to post-harvest spoilage every year — roughly USD 1.3 billion of food destroyed before it reaches a consumer, according to FAO and Ministry of National Food Security & Research estimates. Cold storage doesn't recover all of that, but a well-designed CA store can extend apple shelf life from 4 weeks to 8+ months, capturing the price differential between glut and off-season.

Second, Pakistan's pharmaceutical exports crossed USD 450 million in 2024 and are growing 12% annually — but every shipment to the EU, Middle East, or Africa requires GDP-compliant +2 to +8 °C distribution from factory to airport. Domestic pharma distribution is even more demanding: 220+ million people, two-thirds of them outside the four major metros, all needing temperature-controlled vaccine and biologic supply.

Third, Pakistan's halal meat exports reached USD 460 million in 2024, with poultry exports adding another USD 80 million. Both depend entirely on blast-freezer capacity at the processing plant and frozen-storage capacity at the port. The bottleneck isn't slaughter capacity — it's −25 °C storage waiting at the dock.

Four demand drivers shaping the next five years

1. The post-harvest loss reversal

Pakistan grows 4.6 million tonnes of mangoes, 2.2 million tonnes of citrus, 1.7 million tonnes of dates, and significant volumes of apples, peaches, apricots, and grapes. The country exports a fraction of what it grows — not because demand is lacking but because cold storage capacity at farm gate, in transit, and at consolidation hubs simply doesn't exist at the scale required. Every fruit that goes to market without ever being chilled is, in commodity terms, a discount on the producer's revenue.

Investors who build CA stores in controlled-atmosphere configurations capture a different game entirely: by holding apples at +1 °C with 2.5% O₂ and 2% CO₂, harvest-time pricing becomes a floor rather than a ceiling. KPK apple growers who store through to March-April routinely realise 2–3× the September wholesale price.

2. Pharma compliance is non-negotiable

The Drug Regulatory Authority of Pakistan (DRAP) has tightened storage and distribution requirements every year since 2020. Vaccines, insulin, biologics, and a growing list of branded generics require validated +2 to +8 °C cold chain from manufacturer to dispensary. Distributors who can prove temperature mapping, alarm history, and 24/7 monitoring win the contracts; those who can't are quietly being delisted. Validated pharmaceutical cold rooms with documented qualification packages are now a precondition for serving institutional buyers and exporting under WHO PQ standards.

3. Halal meat and the export-grade freezer gap

Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Malaysia are growing markets for Pakistani halal beef and poultry — but every kilogram shipped requires −25 °C frozen storage at the port, often for 5–14 days while cargo consolidates and shipping is booked. Karachi and Port Qasim operate at freezer capacity ceilings during peak export months. Plate-freezer installations at processing plants and walk-in frozen warehouses near the port are some of the highest-utilisation cold storage assets in the country.

4. Modern retail and quick commerce

Carrefour, Imtiaz, Al-Fatah, Metro, and the new generation of dark stores serving Foodpanda, Krave Mart, and Airlift all need multi-temperature distribution hubs: −18 °C frozen, +4 °C chilled, +18 °C ambient under one roof, with pre-cooled docking. The economics only work if the DC is within 30–60 minutes of the served metro — meaning Lahore Ring Road, M-9 corridor between Karachi and Hyderabad, and Islamabad's GT Road belt are all underserved relative to demand.

The economics: what cold storage actually costs and earns

Construction costs in 2026 PKR (turnkey, including PIR panels, refrigeration plant, doors, and controls):

  • Medium-temperature cold store (+2 to +5 °C): PKR 35,000–55,000 per m³ usable volume.
  • Low-temperature freezer (−18 to −25 °C): PKR 60,000–95,000 per m³.
  • Blast freezer (−40 °C): PKR 90,000–150,000 per m³ — but capacity is rated by tonnes/day throughput, not volume.
  • CA store (+1 °C controlled atmosphere): Roughly 30% above medium-temp cold store cost due to gas-tight envelope and atmosphere control kit.
  • Pharma-validated cold room: 25–40% premium over standard medium-temp construction, primarily for validation, redundancy, and monitoring.

Civil works, ground improvement, dock levellers, and ancillary infrastructure add 15–25% on top. Operating cost — what electricity actually costs

Refrigeration is 70–85% of a cold store's electricity bill. A 2,000 m³ medium-temp cold store at Lahore ambient typically draws 110–160 kWh per day in shoulder months and 250–340 kWh per day in peak summer. At industrial tariffs of PKR 38–52 per kWh (2026), that's PKR 1.5–6 lakh in monthly electricity. The economics turn on three things: panel thickness, refrigeration efficiency (compressor selection, condenser sizing for 50 °C ambient), and door discipline at goods loading.

Where new capacity should go first

Demand is not evenly distributed. The pockets with the largest gap between current capacity and economic demand:

  • KPK and northern Punjab — apple, peach, apricot, and stone-fruit CA stores. Existing capacity is heavily under-built relative to harvest tonnage.
  • Sindh (Khairpur, Sukkur, Hyderabad) — dates and mangoes; export-grade CA capacity for premium varieties going to Gulf and EU markets.
  • Karachi port and Port Qasim — frozen storage feeding meat and seafood export. Constrained during October–February peak shipping.
  • Lahore Ring Road and Lahore–Sialkot corridor — multi-temperature DCs for FMCG, modern retail, and quick commerce serving Lahore's 13 million population.
  • Faisalabad, Sialkot, and Sahiwal — dairy, poultry, and processed-food cold storage close to processing plants.
  • Multan and Bahawalpur — fruit and vegetable consolidation hubs feeding Karachi and Lahore wholesale markets, currently served almost entirely by ambient logistics.

How to evaluate your project

The right project starts with a clear specification of three variables: what you'll store (product + packaging), at what temperature and humidity, and for how long. Those three answers drive panel thickness, refrigeration capacity, door type, and the entire economic case.

From there, the engineering work is concrete:

  1. Heat-load calculation — sum of transmission, infiltration, product, internal, and equipment loads, sized for Pakistan's 50 °C ambient design point. Use our load calculator as a starting check.
  2. Panel selection — thickness from 80 mm (medium-temp) to 200 mm (deep freeze), with PIR core for thermal performance and fire rating. See our PIR sandwich panels page.
  3. Refrigeration architecture — Freon for sub-25 HP commercial loads, ammonia for industrial scale, ammonia-glycol indirect for multi-zone or pharma. The refrigeration systems page covers system types in detail.
  4. Doors, controls, and monitoring — sliding insulated doors, strip curtains, temperature mapping, alarm systems. Often under-budgeted, often the cause of failed audits.
  5. Civil and electrical — slab insulation, drainage, generator backup sized for full plant load.
